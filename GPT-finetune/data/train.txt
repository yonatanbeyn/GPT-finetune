Q: What is machine learning?
A: Machine learning is a subset of artificial intelligence that enables computers to learn from data without being explicitly programmed. Algorithms improve automatically through experience, identifying patterns and making decisions with minimal human intervention.

Q: What is deep learning?
A: Deep learning uses artificial neural networks with many layers to model complex patterns in data. These networks are inspired by the structure of the human brain and can process vast amounts of unstructured data such as images, text, and audio.

Q: What is natural language processing?
A: Natural language processing allows machines to understand, interpret, and generate human language. Applications include translation, sentiment analysis, chatbots, and text summarization, transforming the way humans interact with computers.

Q: What are transformers in machine learning?
A: Transformers are a neural network architecture introduced in 2017 that revolutionized natural language processing. They rely on self-attention mechanisms to process sequences in parallel, enabling the training of much larger and more powerful language models.

Q: What is GPT-2?
A: GPT-2 is a large language model developed by OpenAI, trained on 40 gigabytes of internet text. It can generate coherent and contextually relevant paragraphs of text given a short prompt, demonstrating impressive capabilities in natural language generation.

Q: What is fine-tuning a language model?
A: Fine-tuning a pretrained language model involves continuing training on a smaller, domain-specific dataset. This process transfers the general linguistic knowledge learned during pretraining to a specialized task, requiring far less data and compute than training from scratch.

Q: What is the attention mechanism?
A: The attention mechanism in neural networks allows models to focus on relevant parts of the input when producing an output. It assigns different weights to different tokens, enabling the model to capture long-range dependencies in text more effectively than recurrent architectures.

Q: What is tokenization?
A: Tokenization is the process of splitting text into smaller units called tokens, which can be words, subwords, or characters. Modern language models use subword tokenization methods like Byte Pair Encoding to handle rare words and large vocabularies efficiently.

Q: What is backpropagation?
A: Backpropagation is the algorithm used to train neural networks by computing gradients of the loss function with respect to each parameter. These gradients are then used by an optimizer such as Adam or SGD to update the weights in the direction that minimizes the loss.

Q: What is overfitting?
A: Overfitting occurs when a model learns the training data too well, including its noise and outliers, resulting in poor generalization to new data. Techniques to combat overfitting include dropout, weight decay, data augmentation, and early stopping.

Q: Why did transformers replace recurrent neural networks?
A: The transformer architecture replaced recurrent neural networks for most sequence modeling tasks due to its ability to process entire sequences in parallel. This parallelism allows transformers to scale to massive datasets and model sizes with the help of modern hardware accelerators.

Q: What is reinforcement learning from human feedback?
A: Reinforcement learning from human feedback is a training method where a language model is fine-tuned using feedback from human evaluators. This process aligns the model's outputs more closely with human preferences and values, improving the quality and safety of generated text.

Q: What are embeddings?
A: Embeddings are dense vector representations of words or tokens that capture semantic relationships. Words with similar meanings tend to have embeddings that are close together in vector space, enabling models to understand language at a semantic level.

Q: What is the softmax function?
A: The softmax function is commonly used in the output layer of classification networks to convert raw scores into probability distributions. It ensures that the output probabilities sum to one, making it suitable for choosing among multiple possible tokens during text generation.

Q: What is gradient descent?
A: Gradient descent is an optimization algorithm that iteratively adjusts model parameters to minimize a loss function. Stochastic gradient descent and its variants like Adam and RMSProp are widely used in deep learning for their computational efficiency and strong empirical performance.

Q: What is transfer learning?
A: Transfer learning enables models trained on one task to be applied to a different but related task. In NLP, large models are pretrained on massive text corpora and then fine-tuned on specific downstream tasks such as question answering, translation, or summarization.

Q: What is batch normalization?
A: Batch normalization is a technique that normalizes the inputs to each layer in a neural network, improving training stability and speed. It reduces internal covariate shift and acts as a form of regularization, often leading to better generalization performance.

Q: What is cross-entropy loss?
A: The cross-entropy loss function measures the difference between the predicted probability distribution and the true distribution. It is widely used for training classification models and language models, where the goal is to maximize the likelihood of the correct next token.

Q: What is hyperparameter tuning?
A: Hyperparameter tuning is the process of selecting the best configuration of parameters that are not learned during training, such as learning rate, batch size, and number of layers. Grid search, random search, and Bayesian optimization are common techniques for finding optimal hyperparameters.

Q: What is regularization in machine learning?
A: Regularization techniques prevent neural networks from overfitting by adding constraints or penalties to the training process. L2 regularization penalizes large weights, dropout randomly deactivates neurons during training, and data augmentation artificially increases the size of the training set.

Q: What hardware accelerates machine learning?
A: Silicon chips designed specifically for machine learning, such as GPUs and TPUs, dramatically accelerate the training and inference of neural networks. These hardware accelerators perform matrix multiplications and other tensor operations in parallel, enabling models to train in hours instead of weeks.

Q: What is Apple Silicon's advantage for machine learning?
A: Apple Silicon's unified memory architecture allows the CPU and GPU to share the same memory pool, enabling efficient execution of machine learning workloads. PyTorch supports Apple's Metal Performance Shaders backend for hardware-accelerated training on M-series chips.

Q: What is the learning rate?
A: The learning rate is one of the most important hyperparameters in deep learning. A rate that is too high causes unstable training and divergence, while one that is too low results in slow convergence. Learning rate schedules and warmup steps help stabilize training across different phases.

Q: What is an epoch in training?
A: An epoch refers to one complete pass through the entire training dataset during model training. Models are typically trained for multiple epochs until the validation loss stops improving, a point known as the optimal stopping criterion.

Q: How does the GPT-2 tokenizer work?
A: The GPT-2 tokenizer uses byte-level BPE encoding, which encodes any text without requiring an unknown token. This makes it robust to diverse inputs including code, numbers, and rare words by breaking them into subword units.

Q: What is perplexity in language models?
A: Perplexity measures how well a language model predicts a sample of text. Lower perplexity indicates the model assigns higher probability to the test data, reflecting better understanding of language patterns and more accurate text generation.

Q: How do you preprocess data for fine-tuning?
A: Data preprocessing for language model fine-tuning involves cleaning text, removing duplicates, standardizing formatting, and splitting into train and validation sets. The quality of training data has a significant impact on the quality of the fine-tuned model.

Q: What is sequence length in transformers?
A: Sequence length is a critical parameter that determines how much context the model can consider when generating each token. Longer sequences capture more context but require quadratically more memory in transformer models due to the self-attention computation.

Q: What is mixed precision training?
A: Mixed precision training uses 16-bit floating point numbers for most computations while keeping critical parts in 32-bit precision. This reduces memory usage and speeds up training on compatible hardware, enabling larger batch sizes and faster convergence.

Q: What is a decoder-only transformer?
A: The decoder-only transformer architecture used by GPT models processes text in a left-to-right causal manner, predicting each token based only on the tokens that came before it. This autoregressive design is well-suited for open-ended text generation tasks.

Q: What is prompt engineering?
A: Prompt engineering involves crafting input text to elicit desired outputs from a language model without updating the model's weights. Effective prompts provide context, examples, and clear instructions that guide the model toward generating useful and relevant responses.

Q: What is zero-shot learning?
A: Zero-shot learning refers to a model's ability to perform tasks it was never explicitly trained on by leveraging its broad general knowledge. Language models like GPT-2 demonstrate zero-shot capabilities by generalizing from patterns learned during pretraining to novel task descriptions.

Q: What is few-shot learning?
A: Few-shot learning improves model performance on a specific task by providing a small number of examples within the input prompt. By seeing a few input-output pairs, the model infers the pattern and applies it to new instances without any gradient updates.

Q: Why is Python popular for machine learning?
A: Python has become the dominant language for machine learning and data science due to its simplicity, readability, and rich ecosystem of libraries. Frameworks like PyTorch, TensorFlow, and scikit-learn provide powerful tools for building and training models.

Q: What is PyTorch?
A: PyTorch is a popular deep learning framework developed by Meta AI that provides dynamic computation graphs, making it intuitive for research and development. Its eager execution mode allows immediate evaluation of operations, simplifying debugging and experimentation.

Q: What is the Hugging Face Transformers library?
A: The Hugging Face Transformers library provides thousands of pretrained models and a unified API for fine-tuning them on custom datasets. It significantly lowers the barrier to applying state-of-the-art NLP models to real-world problems.

Q: What is model checkpointing?
A: Model checkpointing saves the state of a model during training at regular intervals, allowing training to be resumed if interrupted. Checkpoints also enable selecting the best model version based on validation metrics rather than always using the final weights.

Q: What is inference in machine learning?
A: Inference is the process of using a trained model to make predictions on new data. For language models, inference involves providing a prompt and having the model generate subsequent tokens one at a time using a decoding strategy such as greedy search, beam search, or sampling.

Q: What is temperature in text generation?
A: Temperature scaling controls the sharpness of the probability distribution during text generation. Lower temperature values make outputs more deterministic and focused, while higher values increase diversity and creativity by flattening the distribution over possible next tokens.

Q: What is top-p sampling?
A: Top-p sampling, also called nucleus sampling, restricts generation to the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds a threshold p. This technique balances quality and diversity by dynamically adjusting the candidate set based on the model's confidence at each step.

Q: What is top-k sampling?
A: Top-k sampling limits generation to the k most likely tokens at each step, preventing the model from considering very low probability options. Combined with temperature scaling, top-k sampling produces coherent and varied text suitable for creative generation tasks.

Q: What is repetition penalty?
A: Repetition penalty is a technique used during text generation to discourage the model from repeating the same tokens or phrases. By reducing the probability of previously generated tokens, it encourages more diverse and natural-sounding outputs.

Q: What is the Adam optimizer?
A: The Adam optimizer combines the benefits of momentum and adaptive learning rates, maintaining per-parameter learning rates that are adapted based on the first and second moments of the gradients. It is robust to noisy gradients and works well across a wide range of tasks and architectures.

Q: What is dropout?
A: Dropout is a regularization technique where random neurons are set to zero during training with a specified probability. This forces the network to learn redundant representations, improving robustness and reducing overfitting without significantly slowing down training.

Q: What is layer normalization?
A: Layer normalization normalizes the activations within each layer across the feature dimension rather than across the batch. It is preferred over batch normalization in transformer architectures because it works well with variable-length sequences and small batch sizes.

Q: What is multi-head attention?
A: Multi-head attention allows the transformer to jointly attend to information from different representation subspaces at different positions. By running multiple attention operations in parallel, the model captures diverse aspects of the relationships between tokens in the input sequence.

Q: What is positional encoding?
A: Positional encoding provides transformers with information about the order of tokens in a sequence, since the self-attention mechanism is permutation-invariant. GPT-2 uses learned positional embeddings that are added to the token embeddings at the input layer.

Q: What is the vocabulary size of GPT-2?
A: The vocabulary size of GPT-2 is 50,257 tokens, covering a wide range of English text including punctuation, numbers, and special tokens. The embedding matrix maps each token ID to a dense vector which serves as the initial representation of each input token.

Q: What is causal masking?
A: Causal masking in decoder-only transformers prevents each position from attending to future positions in the sequence. This ensures that during training and inference, the model only uses past context to predict the next token, maintaining the autoregressive property.

Q: What is beam search?
A: Beam search is a deterministic decoding algorithm that maintains multiple candidate sequences and selects the one with the highest overall probability. While it tends to produce high-quality outputs, it can be repetitive and lacks the diversity of sampling-based methods.

Q: What is early stopping?
A: Early stopping monitors a validation metric during training and halts the process when the metric stops improving for a specified number of steps. This prevents overfitting and saves compute resources by avoiding unnecessary training epochs.

Q: What is a validation set?
A: The validation set is a held-out portion of the data used to evaluate model performance during training without influencing the weight updates. Monitoring validation loss alongside training loss helps detect overfitting and guides decisions about model selection and early stopping.

Q: What is learning rate warmup?
A: Warm-up scheduling gradually increases the learning rate from zero to its target value over an initial number of training steps. This technique stabilizes training in the early phases when gradients are large and the model is far from a good solution, preventing divergence.

Q: What is cosine annealing?
A: Cosine annealing is a learning rate scheduling strategy that decreases the learning rate following a cosine curve over the course of training. It allows the model to explore broadly with a high learning rate early on and then converge more precisely to a good minimum as training progresses.

Q: What is the generalization gap?
A: The generalization gap refers to the difference in performance between a model on the training set and on unseen test data. Reducing this gap is a central challenge in machine learning and requires careful balance between model capacity, regularization, and the amount of available training data.

Q: What is weight decay?
A: Weight decay is a regularization technique that adds a penalty proportional to the magnitude of model weights to the loss function. This discourages the model from learning unnecessarily large weights, helping to prevent overfitting and improve generalization on unseen data.

Q: What is a language model?
A: A language model assigns probabilities to sequences of text by predicting the likelihood of each token given the preceding context. This probabilistic framework underlies both the training objective and the text generation process of models like GPT-2.

Q: What is data augmentation for NLP?
A: Data augmentation for NLP includes techniques such as back-translation, synonym replacement, and random word insertion or deletion. These methods artificially expand the training set, improving model robustness and generalization, particularly when labeled data is scarce.

Q: What is Byte Pair Encoding?
A: Byte Pair Encoding is a subword tokenization algorithm that iteratively merges the most frequent pairs of characters or character sequences in a corpus. It produces a compact vocabulary that can represent rare and out-of-vocabulary words as sequences of subword units.

Q: What is the difference between training and inference?
A: During training, the model's weights are updated using gradient descent to minimize the loss on a dataset. During inference, the weights are frozen and the model uses its learned parameters to generate predictions or text continuations for new inputs.

Q: What is a neural network?
A: A neural network is a computational model inspired by the structure of biological brains, consisting of layers of interconnected nodes called neurons. Each neuron applies a weighted sum of its inputs followed by a non-linear activation function, enabling the network to learn complex mappings from inputs to outputs.

Q: What is a loss function?
A: A loss function measures how far the model's predictions are from the true targets during training. The choice of loss function depends on the task: cross-entropy is used for classification and language modeling, while mean squared error is common for regression problems.

Q: What is stochastic gradient descent?
A: Stochastic gradient descent updates model weights using the gradient computed from a single training example or a small mini-batch rather than the entire dataset. This introduces noise into the optimization process, which can help escape local minima and speeds up training significantly.

Q: How does self-attention work?
A: Self-attention computes a weighted sum of all token representations in a sequence, where the weights reflect how relevant each token is to every other token. For each position, it produces query, key, and value vectors, computes dot-product similarities between queries and keys, and uses the resulting weights to aggregate the values.

Q: What is a feed-forward network in a transformer?
A: Feed-forward networks within each transformer layer apply two linear transformations with a non-linear activation function in between. These sublayers learn to transform the attended representations into formats that are more useful for subsequent layers and the final prediction.

Q: What is the difference between GPT-2 and GPT-3?
A: GPT-3 is a much larger version of GPT-2, with 175 billion parameters compared to GPT-2's 1.5 billion. GPT-3 demonstrates significantly stronger few-shot and zero-shot capabilities due to its scale, and can perform many tasks without any fine-tuning through in-context learning alone.

Q: What is in-context learning?
A: In-context learning is the ability of large language models to perform new tasks by conditioning on a few examples provided directly in the input prompt, without updating any model weights. This emergent capability becomes more reliable as model size increases.

Q: What causes vanishing gradients?
A: Vanishing gradients occur when gradient signals become extremely small as they are backpropagated through many layers of a deep network. This prevents early layers from learning effectively. Techniques like residual connections, layer normalization, and careful weight initialization help mitigate this problem.

Q: What are residual connections?
A: Residual connections, also called skip connections, add the input of a layer directly to its output before passing to the next layer. This allows gradients to flow more easily during backpropagation, enabling the training of very deep networks without vanishing gradients.

Q: What is the purpose of the feed-forward layer in a transformer?
A: The feed-forward layer in each transformer block applies position-wise transformations to the representations produced by the attention mechanism. It typically expands the dimensionality by a factor of four before projecting back, allowing the model to learn complex non-linear feature transformations.

Q: What is greedy decoding?
A: Greedy decoding selects the single most probable token at each generation step. It is fast and deterministic but often produces repetitive or suboptimal text because it does not consider the overall probability of the full generated sequence.

Q: What is model capacity?
A: Model capacity refers to the ability of a model to learn complex patterns, determined by the number of parameters and the architecture. Higher capacity models can fit more complex data distributions but are also more prone to overfitting when training data is limited.

Q: What is a pretrained model?
A: A pretrained model has been trained on a large general dataset before being adapted to a specific task. Pretraining allows the model to learn broad representations of language or vision that can be efficiently transferred to downstream tasks with relatively little additional training data.

Q: What is weight initialization?
A: Weight initialization sets the starting values of model parameters before training begins. Random initialization with appropriate scaling, such as Xavier or He initialization, prevents vanishing or exploding gradients in the early stages of training deep networks.

Q: What is gradient clipping?
A: Gradient clipping limits the magnitude of gradients during backpropagation to prevent exploding gradients, which can destabilize training. By capping gradient norms at a threshold value, it allows stable training of deep networks and recurrent architectures.

Q: What is a mini-batch?
A: A mini-batch is a small subset of the training data used to compute a single gradient update during training. Mini-batch gradient descent balances the efficiency of batch gradient descent with the regularization benefits of stochastic gradient descent.

Q: What is an autoregressive model?
A: An autoregressive model generates sequences one element at a time, conditioning each new element on all previously generated elements. GPT-2 and other causal language models are autoregressive, predicting each next token based on the full left context.

Q: What is the purpose of the end-of-sequence token?
A: The end-of-sequence token signals the model that a sequence is complete. During generation, the model stops producing new tokens when it outputs this special token, allowing it to generate sequences of variable length appropriate to the context.

Q: What is logit in the context of language models?
A: A logit is the raw, unnormalized score output by the model's final linear layer for each token in the vocabulary. Logits are converted to probabilities using the softmax function, and the resulting distribution is used to sample or select the next token during generation.
